## What you will use

- Barbells, bumper plates, and platforms: the full strength base, plus the ability to pull fast and drop the bar, which is what makes olympic lifting possible
- Squat racks and a pull-up rig: to squat and press heavy with safeties, and to train pull-ups, dips, and hangs from a dedicated structure
- A GHD and an ab-mat: for back extensions, hip extensions, and the strongest posterior-chain and trunk work in any gym
- Sleds, and a turf lane or track to push them on: the most joint-friendly heavy conditioning there is, all concentric, no soreness
- Rowers and air bikes: the precise engine machines for Zone 2 and for the hardest intervals in fitness
- A complete bell and dumbbell range, rings, and a dip station: every accessory the home tiers used, in every size

## For the ruck

- A weight vest or a loaded pack: the Day 5 ruck is done outdoors when you can, or on a treadmill at a steep incline when you cannot. Carry the keystone outside the four walls whenever the weather allows

## The honest gap

There isn't one. C2 is the top of the Stead ladder, the equal of Tier 3 with more toys and more company. The only thing beyond it is specialization: a dedicated weightlifting hall, a powerlifting gym, a velodrome, a track, for a person training for one specific sport. For a full-spectrum, durable, operator-and-longevity body, this room is the ceiling. From here the variable is not the gear. It is the patience to use it well.

> **A NOTE ON THE CULTURE OF THE ROOM**
> The best and worst thing about a serious gym is the energy around you. It will push you to work harder than you would alone, which is a gift, and it will push you to redline on days this program asks you to go easy, which is a trap. Wear headphones on the Zone 2 days. Let the class chase the leaderboard while you build the base underneath it. The fittest forty-year-old in the room got there by knowing which days to lose.
