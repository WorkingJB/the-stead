## Strength assessments

Take a full rest day before testing. Work up in small jumps to a strong, honest top set and stop the moment form degrades. These are not one-rep-max attempts; they are clean sets that tell you what your training built. The squat and the deadlift are the headline numbers of this tier, expressed relative to your bodyweight because that ratio is what carries over to moving yourself and a load through the world.

## Aerobic assessments

Run these on separate days from the strength tests and from each other. They are efforts, not races; hold a pace you could sustain. The ruck standards are the ones that matter most for what the program is ultimately for.

> **IF YOU HIT YOUR PATH'S STANDARDS**
> You have reached the top of the home path. A Path B trainee at these numbers has a credible base for special operations selection prep and a profile that will defend independence for life. From here the work is to maintain and to keep training; there is no Tier 4. If you want a fuller facility or a community to train with, the commercial tiers run this same arc in a real gym.

> **IF YOU DIDN'T HIT THEM YET**
> Run Block 3 again, or run the whole tier again, adding small amounts of weight to the lifts you fell short on. Pick the two assessments that lagged and concentrate volume there. Most people who repeat a block hit standards the second time. The Stead trains people who plan to keep training for forty years; one extra month is not a setback, it's a calibration.
