## Barbell Deadlift
**LOADED HINGE · BOTH PATHS**

**Why it matters · Operator**
The deadlift is picking the heaviest possible load off the ground with a safe back, which is the most fundamental strength task there is. It builds the entire posterior chain, the grip, and the bracing strength behind every heavy lift, carry, and drag. No movement transfers more directly to moving real weight in the real world.

**Why it matters · Longevity**
Picking heavy things up off the floor is the single most-rehearsed and most-injured task of aging life. Training the deadlift grooves a braced, safe, powerful hinge under load, so the pattern holds when life demands it unexpectedly. Strong glutes and hamstrings are also the best insurance against the low-back pain that sidelines so many older adults.

**Form cues**
- Bar over the mid-foot, shins close; hinge and grip just outside the legs
- Set the back flat and the lats tight, hips between the knees and the shoulders, chest up
- Take the slack out of the bar, then drive the floor away; the bar drags up the legs
- Stand tall by finishing with the hips, glutes squeezed; do not lean back at the top
- Lower by pushing the hips back first, the bar tracking down the legs; reset each rep

**Common errors**
- Rounding the lower back to rip the bar off the floor (set the flat back and brace before pulling)
- The hips shooting up first, turning it into a stiff-legged pull (drive with the legs and hips together)
- Jerking the bar from a dead stop without taking out the slack (pull the slack, then go)

**Path A scaling**
Build with moderate loads and higher reps, technique and a flat back before weight. The trap-bar or a dumbbell deadlift are excellent, more forgiving entry points. Reset every rep; never bounce a heavy bar off the floor.

**Path B scaling**
Train the deadlift heavy at low reps, progressing the load in small steps. Because it is so taxing, keep the volume modest and the form pristine; this is one lift where ego costs the most. Cycle tempo and pause work to sharpen the pull off the floor.

## Romanian Deadlift
**HINGE + HAMSTRING · BOTH PATHS**

**Why it matters · Operator**
The Romanian deadlift trains the hinge through a long hamstring stretch under load, building the eccentric strength that protects the back and powers every hip extension, from the sprint to the casualty drag. It is the accessory that keeps the heavy deadlift healthy and the posterior chain balanced.

**Why it matters · Longevity**
Hamstring and glute strength decline faster than almost any other muscle group after age 50, and the decline tracks falls, low-back pain, and difficulty rising from chairs. The Romanian deadlift loads exactly that tissue through a full range, building the flexible, strong posterior chain that keeps an older body moving freely.

**Form cues**
- Start standing tall with the bar (or dumbbells) at the hips, a soft bend in the knees
- Push the hips straight back, letting the bar travel down the thighs and shins
- Keep the back flat and the bar close; feel the hamstrings load and lengthen
- Lower until you feel a strong stretch (usually mid-shin), not until the back rounds
- Drive the hips forward to stand tall, squeezing the glutes at the top

**Common errors**
- Bending the knees and turning it into a squat (the knees stay mostly fixed; the hips move)
- Letting the bar drift away from the legs (keep it dragging close to protect the back)
- Chasing depth past the hamstring's range until the lower back rounds

**Path A scaling**
Use dumbbells or a moderate barbell load, prioritizing the hip hinge and a flat back over depth or weight. Build the pattern until the hinge is automatic before loading it hard.

**Path B scaling**
Load the barbell Romanian deadlift moderately heavy for controlled reps, and add a slow eccentric or a pause at the stretch. Keep it lighter than the conventional deadlift; its job is quality and hamstring strength, not a max.
