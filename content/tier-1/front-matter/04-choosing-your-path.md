## Path A · The on-ramp

For people arriving from Tier 0, new to the kettlebell, or returning to loaded training after time away. Path A starts with lighter ballistic volume, assisted or negative pullups, and shorter rucks, but follows the same block structure and arrives at the same Tier 2 readiness by week twelve. If you cannot yet do a strict pullup, this is your path; closing that gap is one of its explicit goals.

You're on Path A if:

- You cannot yet do three to five strict pullups
- The kettlebell swing and goblet squat are new or rusty patterns for you
- A 60-minute ruck at 20 lb is honest work, not easy
- You are arriving fresh from Tier 0 and want to learn the new tools first
- You're returning from a layoff and rebuilding loaded capacity

## Path B · The build

For people with a real training base who own the pullup and want to be deliberate about building toward the SOF and longevity intersection. Path B uses the heavier bell, single-arm ballistics, weighted pullups, and longer loaded rucks from the start, but holds the same 80/20 discipline and deload schedule. Path B at week twelve is a credible candidate for Tier 2.

You're on Path B if:

- You can do five or more strict pullups today
- The swing, goblet squat, and Romanian deadlift are grooved patterns
- A 75-minute ruck at 25 lb is comfortable
- You have at least six months of recent consistent training, including some loaded work
- You're injury-free and ready for honest volume

> **WHEN IN DOUBT, START ON PATH A**
> The cost of starting too easy is one week of feeling under-challenged before you graduate up. The cost of starting too hard is an overuse injury, a strained back from a heavy swing, or quitting in week three. Path A is not "the worse path." It is the path that respects what your tissues can actually absorb right now.
