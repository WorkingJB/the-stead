## Required (in addition to your Tier 1 kit)

- A second kettlebell, matched to your first: the centerpiece upgrade. A matched pair unlocks the double clean, the double front squat, and the double press, which is how you finally load the legs and shoulders past what one bell allows. Most people want a second bell equal to their working bell; sizing a hair lighter is fine while the patterns are new
- Gymnastics rings: the single most versatile tool in the kit. Hung from the pullup bar, a beam, or a tree, they turn the dip and the row into scalable, joint-friendly strength movements and add a stability demand no fixed bar can. Wood or plastic both work; you mostly need sturdy straps and a solid anchor
- A sandbag: an adjustable training bag, 40-80 lb of capacity, filled with sand in inner bags. The awkward, shifting load is the point. It trains the clean, the carry, and the over-shoulder that a rigid bell never will
- A jump rope: a few dollars, and the most efficient conditioning and footwork tool there is. It builds calf and foot resilience, coordination, and a portable interval engine
- A plyo box, or any sturdy knee-to-mid-thigh platform: for box jumps (power), step-ups (loaded single-leg strength), and feet-elevated work

## Strongly recommended (under $40 total)

- A dip belt or a sturdy backpack: for loading weighted pullups and weighted ring dips in Block 3, once bodyweight is easy
- A second anchor height for the rings: low for rows, high for dips and pull work
- Chalk: the double bells and the sandbag both punish a sweaty grip

## The honest gap

Tier 2 has one structural limit: no dedicated cardio machine and no continuous-load progression past the bells and the bag. With two bells and a sandbag you can load most patterns heavily, but the very heaviest lower-body work, and the fine-grained dumbbell jumps, are still out of reach. We work around this with double-bell complexes, tempo, and the sandbag. It is enough for a year or more of honest progress. When the double front squat and the sandbag stop challenging your legs and you want a rower, a full dumbbell range, and a real barbell, that is the signal for Tier 3.

> **A NOTE ON THE SECOND BELL**
> Resist the urge to buy the second bell heavier than your first to "have room to grow." Two matched bells you can clean and rack cleanly will serve you for the whole program; a pair you cannot rack will gather dust. The double front squat humbles people who were strong with one bell. Earn it before you load it.
