## Path A · The on-ramp

For people new to the gym, returning after a long layoff, or unsure how to use the floor with a plan. Path A leans on the machines, where the path of the weight is guided and a hard set is safe to take alone, and on lighter dumbbells and the assisted pull-up station. It follows the same block structure and arrives at real free-weight competence and a first pull-up by week twelve. If the dumbbell rack and the squat rack still feel like someone else's territory, this is your path; making the whole floor yours is one of its explicit goals.

You're on Path A if:

- You are new to the gym, or returning after time away
- The machines feel safer to you than the free-weight area right now
- You cannot yet do a strict pull-up
- You want a clear plan more than you want heavy numbers
- You are arriving from a couch, not from another program

## Path B · The build

For people comfortable on the free-weight floor who own the basic patterns and want to be deliberate about building toward the SOF and longevity intersection. Path B leans on the heavier dumbbells, the free-weight and cable work, and weighted or strict pull-ups, but holds the same 80/20 discipline and deload schedule. Path B uses the machines as accessories rather than anchors. Path B at week twelve is a credible candidate for C2 or for the home garage.

You're on Path B if:

- You move confidently around the dumbbell rack and the free-weight area
- You can do five or more strict pull-ups
- A 75-minute ruck at 25 lb is comfortable
- You have at least six months of recent consistent training
- You're injury-free and ready for honest volume

> **WHEN IN DOUBT, START ON PATH A**
> The cost of starting too easy is one week of feeling under-challenged before you graduate up. The cost of starting too hard is an overuse injury or quitting in week three. Path A is not "the worse path." It is the path that respects what your tissues can actually absorb right now. The machines are not a lesser tool; they are how you earn the free weights safely.
