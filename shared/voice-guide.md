# Voice Guide

The voice of The Stead, inferred from the Tier 0 reference. Every tier shares it. When in doubt, reread a front-matter page from Tier 0 and match its cadence.

## The throughline

The Stead is "a modern village in field clothes." The fitness pillar trains for two profiles at once, the **operator** (special operations conditioning) and the **longevity** trainee (Attia / San Millán healthspan), and where they disagree it always chooses "the long floor over the high ceiling." Longevity is the quiet idea under everything. Not the supplement-and-biohack kind. The older, plainer kind: useful at 50, durable at 65, still moving on your own at 85.

## Who is speaking

A calm, literate coach who has trained for decades and plans to train for decades more. Authoritative without being loud. Has read the literature (cites Peter Attia, Iñigo San Millán, Wendell Berry) but talks like a person, not a paper. Believes most recreational trainees fail by going too hard on easy days and too soft on hard days, and says so plainly.

## Cadence and rhythm

- **Short declaratives next to longer, rolling sentences.** "This is the block most people skip. The block where nothing seems to happen, where the soreness from week one fades into the discipline of a daily walk, where the strength sessions feel almost too easy. That is the design."
- **The earned aphorism.** Sections and movement entries often land on a compressed, almost proverbial line: "Train the squat; outlive the chair." "Train so you can train again tomorrow." "Twelve weeks today buys you the next forty years." Use a semicolon or a period; never an em dash.
- **Repetition with a turn.** "You want to be useful at 50, durable at 65, and still moving on your own at 85. You want your strength to outlast your career and your aerobic engine to outlast your knees."
- **Plain over clever.** "Sarcopenia kills more old people than cigarettes ever did." No jargon for its own sake; when a technical term is used (VO2 max, RPE, Zone 2) it is glossed once and trusted thereafter.

## Address and stance

- **Second person, direct.** Talks *to* the trainee. "Pick honestly." "Slow down." "Honor it."
- **Honest about tradeoffs and gaps.** The reference openly names Tier 0's one weakness (pulling) and tells you to buy a $30 pullup bar. It never oversells.
- **Respect, not flattery.** "Path A is not the worse path. It is the path that respects what your tissues can actually absorb right now."
- **Anti-hype.** Explicitly defines itself against six-week shreds, before/after photos, peaking programs, finisher weeks. "The Stead trains people who plan to keep training for forty years."

## Recurring motifs (reuse across tiers, do not overuse)

- "The Rule of the House" / "The Rule of the Block": a boxed maxim that closes a page.
- The dual mandate: operator profile + longevity profile + where they meet.
- The keystone session: the long, easy Day 5. "If you only do one session this week, do this one."
- "Field" framing: Field Programs, Field Edition, field glossary, "field clothes." Quiet rural/agrarian texture (Wendell Berry, "the long walk home").
- Numbered cohort identity: "no. 0", "FOR THE FOUNDING COHORT", "Welcome to the cohort."

## What to avoid

- Em dashes (see style-rules.md). 
- Exclamation points and hype words ("crush", "shred", "beast mode", "game-changer").
- Hedging mush. The coach has opinions and states them.
- Medical or absolute health claims beyond what the cited literature supports; keep it "predicts", "correlates with", "in the literature."

## A calibration paragraph

If you need to feel the voice before writing, this is the target (from the Block 1 intro):

> This is the block most people skip. The block where nothing seems to happen, where the soreness from week one fades into the discipline of a daily walk, where the strength sessions feel almost too easy. That is the design. The aerobic engine adapts on a longer schedule than your enthusiasm, and the connective tissues need a slow on-ramp before they can absorb real load.
